## Key Concepts Learned

### Concept 1: Primary Market (ä¸€çº§å¸‚åœº) vs Secondary Market (äºŒçº§å¸‚åœº)

**Definition/Explanation**:
- **Primary Market** = Company issues NEW securities, capital flows TO the company
- **Secondary Market** = Investors trade EXISTING securities among themselves, capital flows between investors

**Key Points**:
- **Primary Market**:
  - Also called "New Issue Market"
  - Includes IPO, Follow-on Offerings, Private Placements
  - Company receives capital from these transactions

- **Secondary Market**:
  - Also called "Trading Market"
  - NYSE and NASDAQ are secondary markets
  - Company does NOT receive money from these trades
  - Capital flows between investors only

**Real-World Example**:
Tesla IPO in 2010 at $17/share â†’ Primary Market (Tesla got the money)
Buying Tesla stock today at $250/share â†’ Secondary Market (money goes to other investor, not Tesla)

**Critical Insight for SIE**:
- Company only raises capital in Primary Market
- Secondary market stock price changes don't directly bring cash to the company
- However, high stock price helps company indirectly (easier to raise more capital, use stock for acquisitions, etc.)

**Related Concepts**:
- Underwriting process
- IPO vs Follow-on Offerings
- Stock exchanges as secondary markets

### Concept 2: NYSE vs NASDAQ - Market Structure Differences

**Definition/Explanation**:
- **NYSE** = Auction Market with physical trading floor and Designated Market Makers
- **NASDAQ** = Dealer Market with fully electronic trading and multiple competing market makers

**Key Points**:

| Feature | NYSE | NASDAQ |
|---------|------|--------|
| Market Type | Auction Market | Dealer Market |
| Trading Method | Physical floor + Electronic | Fully Electronic |
| Market Makers | DMMs (1 per stock) | Multiple Market Makers compete |
| Listing Requirements | Stricter (higher revenue/profit requirements) | More flexible (allows unprofitable companies) |
| Typical Companies | Traditional large enterprises | Tech/growth companies |
| Founded | 1792 | 1971 |

**Real-World Example**:
- Tesla IPO in 2010: Unprofitable at the time â†’ Listed on NASDAQ (more flexible requirements)
- Coca-Cola, JPMorgan: Mature profitable companies â†’ Listed on NYSE
- Companies can switch exchanges (e.g., Kraft moved from NASDAQ to NYSE)

**For Investors**:
- Buying stocks on NYSE vs NASDAQ feels identical for retail investors
- Same price transparency, liquidity, and execution speed
- **SIE focus**: Understanding market structure, not judging which is "better"

**Related Concepts**:
- Market makers and liquidity provision
- Listing requirements and corporate governance
- Exchange competition

### Concept 3: Stock Buybacks (è‚¡ç¥¨å›è´­)

**Definition/Explanation**:
Company uses its cash to repurchase its own shares from investors in the secondary market, then typically cancels those shares.

**Key Points**:
- **Occurs in Secondary Market** (company buys from investors)
- **Effects**:
  - âœ… Reduces number of shares outstanding
  - âœ… Increases EPS (Earnings Per Share)
  - âœ… Tax advantage for shareholders (no immediate tax vs dividend taxation)
  - âœ… More flexible than dividends (can stop anytime)
  - âœ… Signals management believes stock is undervalued

**Buyback vs Dividend Comparison**:

| Aspect | Dividend | Stock Buyback |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| Tax | Shareholders taxed immediately | No tax unless shareholder sells |
| Mandatory | All shareholders receive it | Shareholders choose to sell or not |
| Flexibility | Hard to reduce once started | Can stop anytime |
| Signal | "Stable earnings, ongoing commitment" | "Stock is undervalued" |

**Real-World Example**:
