### Concept 2: Preferred Stock vs Common Stock | ä¼˜å…ˆè‚¡ vs æ™®é€šè‚¡

**Definition**:
- **Preferred Stock (ä¼˜å…ˆè‚¡)**: Equity security with fixed dividend and priority over common stock, but typically no voting rights
- **Common Stock (æ™®é€šè‚¡)**: Basic equity ownership with voting rights and variable dividends, but junior claim

**Key Points**:
- **"Preferred" = Priority in money** (dividends + liquidation), NOT voting
- **"Common" = Common people vote** (voting rights), but last in line for money
- Preferred Stock = "Stock that acts like a bond" (fixed income)
- Common Stock = True ownership with unlimited upside potential

**Complete Comparison Table**:

| Feature | Common Stock | Preferred Stock |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Dividend** | Variable, board decides | Fixed rate (e.g., 6% of par) |
| **Dividend Priority** | Last (after Preferred) | First (before Common) |
| **Voting Rights** | Yes (1 share = 1 vote) | Usually NO |
| **Liquidation Priority** | Last (residual claim) | Before Common, after bonds |
| **Price Volatility** | High | Lower (more stable) |
| **Growth Potential** | Unlimited upside | Limited (fixed income focus) |
| **Suitable For** | Growth investors | Income investors |

**For SIE Exam**:
- Typical question: "Which stockholders receive dividends first?"
- Key trap: Preferred usually has NO voting rights (not "more" voting rights)

### Concept 3: Cumulative vs Non-Cumulative | ç´¯ç§¯ vs éžç´¯ç§¯

**Definition**:
- **Cumulative Preferred**: Unpaid dividends accumulate as "arrears" and must be paid before common dividends
- **Non-Cumulative Preferred**: Unpaid dividends expire - no obligation to make up missed payments

**Key Points**:
- Cumulative = Better investor protection (company must catch up on missed dividends)
- Non-Cumulative = Favors company (missed dividends are gone forever)
- Cumulative preferred typically pays **lower** dividend rate (better protection = lower yield)

**Real-World Example**:
ABC Company has preferred stock with $5/year dividend. Company doesn't pay for 2 years due to losses.

**Year 3, company recovers and wants to pay dividends**:
- **If Cumulative**: Must pay $5(Y1) + $5(Y2) + $5(Y3) = **$15** to preferred before ANY to common
- **If Non-Cumulative**: Only pays current year $5, past $10 is **forfeited**

**For SIE Exam**:
- Calculation question: "How much must cumulative preferred receive if dividends were skipped for X years?"
- Formula: (Annual dividend Ã— # of years skipped) + current year dividend

### Concept 4: Callable vs Non-Callable | å¯èµŽå›ž vs ä¸å¯èµŽå›ž

**Definition**:
- **Callable Preferred**: Company has right to buy back (redeem) stock at predetermined call price
- **Non-Callable Preferred**: Company cannot force redemption - investor keeps stock indefinitely

**Key Points**:
- **Callable = Favors company** (can refinance when rates drop)
- **Non-Callable = Favors investor** (locks in income stream)
- Callable preferred pays **higher** dividend (compensates for reinvestment risk)
- Call typically happens when interest rates decline

**Real-World Example**:
**2020**: Company issues Callable Preferred at 5% dividend, call price = $105
**2025**: Market rates drop to 3%

**Company's decision**:
- Calls back old 5% preferred (pays $105/share)
- Issues new 3% preferred
- **Saves 2% annually on dividends**

**Investor's problem**:
- Lost 5% income stream
- Can only reinvest at 3% now (Reinvestment Risk)

**For SIE Exam**:
- Scenario question: "Why would company call preferred stock?" (Answer: Interest rates declined)
- Key concept: Callable preferred has higher dividend to compensate for call risk

## Common Misconceptions | å¸¸è§è¯¯è§£

### Misconception 1: "Preferred" means more voting power
**Wrong**: Preferred stockholders have MORE voting rights than common stockholders
**Right**: Preferred stockholders typically have NO voting rights
**Why the confusion**: "Preferred" sounds like "better in every way," but it only means priority for money (dividends and liquidation), not control

### Misconception 2: Higher dividend always means better investment
**Wrong**: Stock Y pays 7% vs Stock X's 4%, so Y is better
**Right**: Must examine ALL features - high dividend often compensates for unfavorable features (non-cumulative, callable, non-convertible)
**Why the confusion**: People focus on yield number without considering risk factors

### Misconception 3: Convertible preferred always better than non-convertible
**Wrong**: Convertible is always the best choice
**Right**: Depends on investor needs - retirees may prefer non-convertible with higher dividend over conversion feature they won't use
**Why the confusion**: Conversion seems like "free option," but you pay for it through lower dividend

### Question 1 (Concept Recall)
In your own words, explain what makes Preferred Stock "preferred" and what stockholders give up in exchange.

**Expected Understanding**:
- Preferred = priority for dividends and liquidation (money comes first)
- Give up = voting rights (no say in company decisions)

---

### Question 2 (Application)
A company had financial difficulties and didn't pay preferred dividends for 3 years. This year they recovered. They have both cumulative and non-cumulative preferred stock outstanding. Explain what happens when they want to resume paying dividends.

**Expected Understanding**:
- Cumulative: Must pay all 3 years of arrears + current year before ANY to common
- Non-Cumulative: Past 3 years forfeited, only pay current year
- This demonstrates the protection difference between the two types

---

### Question 3 (Investor Matching)
A 65-year-old retiree needs $50,000 annual income for living expenses. Would you recommend callable or non-callable preferred stock? Why?

**Expected Understanding**:
- Recommend: Non-callable (even with lower dividend)
- Reason: Retiree needs reliable long-term income stream
- Callable risk: If called back, loses income source and faces reinvestment risk
- Retiree can't afford income disruption

### Scenario 1: Comparing Investment Options

**Context**:
You have $10,000 to invest and are comparing two preferred stocks from the same utility company:

**Stock A**:
- Cumulative, Non-Callable, Non-Convertible
- Annual dividend: 6.5%
- Current price: $100/share

**Stock B**:
- Cumulative, Non-Callable, Convertible (ratio: 4)
- Annual dividend: 4.5%
- Current price: $100/share
- Current common stock price: $20

**Questions**:
1. Which provides higher current income?
2. Why does Stock B pay lower dividend?
3. At what common stock price would Stock B become more valuable?
4. Which would you choose if you're 30 years old with stable job? Which if you're 70 and retired?

**Discussion Points**:
- Stock A: $650/year income, no growth potential
- Stock B: $450/year income, but conversion option at $25/share breakeven
- Young investor (30): Stock B better - time for company to grow, can afford lower income now
- Retired (70): Stock A better - needs higher income now, less concerned about growth

### Scenario 2: Callable Preferred During Rate Changes

**Context**:
2020: You bought callable preferred stock at par ($100), 7% dividend, call price $103
2025: Market interest rates dropped from 7% to 4%

**Questions**:
1. Why did the company issue callable (vs non-callable) preferred?
2. Will the company likely call this stock? Why?
3. If called, what's your problem?
4. Did you "lose money" if called at $103?

**Discussion Points**:
- Company benefits from callable: Can refinance when rates drop (7% â†’ 4% saves 3% annually)
- Investor faces reinvestment risk: Got $103, but can only reinvest at 4% now (lost 7% income stream)
- Technically gained $3 on call premium, but lost future high-yield income
- This is why callable preferred must pay higher dividend initially
